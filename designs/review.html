Taxi Driver

Directed by Martin Scorsese

Review by Carrie Gorringe ◆ Published 1995 ◆ USA, 1976  ·  113 min.

Travis Bickle does not sleep. He prowls the nocturnal boroughs of New York City in his taxicab, a self-appointed sentinel in a city he experiences as a sewer — teeming, corrupt, irredeemable. In Martin Scorsese's Taxi Driver, written with methodical psychological precision by Paul Schrader, this insomniac drift through the 1970s urban landscape becomes one of American cinema's most disquieting self-portraits: a meditation on alienation, violence, and the seductive grammar of the vigilante myth.

Robert De Niro's performance is the film's terrifying center of gravity. He never plays Travis as merely disturbed; he plays him as earnest — a quality far more unsettling than simple menace. Travis believes he is seeing clearly. The voiceover journals that structure the film render his worldview with the calm confidence of a man who has located, at last, his purpose. That the purpose is delusional does not diminish his conviction, and it is conviction, finally, that the film interrogates.

Scorsese transforms the 1970s New York cityscape into a fever dream — half confession, half condemnation — lit by neon and scored by Bernard Herrmann's final, haunting masterwork.

— Carrie Gorringe, Nitrate Online

The City as Moral Landscape

Michael Chapman's cinematography is essential to the film's argument. His New York does not merely host the narrative — it generates it. Steam rises from manhole covers as though the earth itself is feverish. Rain slicks every surface to a lurid gloss. Scorsese, returning to the city of his youth, is simultaneously documenting and mythologizing: this is New York as expressionist nightmare, its geography as morally legible as any Bresson landscape.

Bernard Herrmann's final score — completed days before his death — completes the alchemy. The jazz-inflected main theme, humid with saxophone, encloses the film in a kind of urban sadness that transcends genre. It is music that mourns and menaces simultaneously, acknowledging the tragedy of what Travis cannot see about himself.

The Politics of Salvation

The film's most disturbing formal move is its refusal to condemn. Paul Schrader's script — drawn in part from the published diaries of Arthur Bremer, who shot George Wallace — grants Travis a coherence of intention that forecloses easy dismissal. His decision to rescue Iris (Jodie Foster, in a performance of startling maturity) is in one reading genuine altruism; in another, it is merely a socially sanctioned outlet for violence that has always been looking for permission.

Scorsese has acknowledged the film's moral discomfort, and it is precisely that discomfort that gives Taxi Driver its enduring power. The celebrated mirror scene — "You talkin' to me?" — has become cultural shorthand for a certain kind of masculine narcissism, but in context it is something stranger and more frightening: a man practicing the theater of confrontation with an audience of one, auditioning for a role in a script only he can read.

The film's notorious final act — Travis's bloodbath, the media's canonization of him as hero — was, for 1976, a provocation. Forty years on, it reads as prophecy. Taxi Driver understood, before most American films dared to, that the culture's appetite for the lone avenger and its capacity for mass violence were not opposites but expressions of the same pathology.

A Film That Refuses to Flinch

What separates Taxi Driver from the revenge genre it superficially resembles is Scorsese's absolute refusal of catharsis. The violence, when it comes, is not satisfying; it is nauseating, shot in slow motion with a detachment that approximates Travis's own dissociation. We are not permitted to enjoy it. We are, instead, made to examine our own desire for resolution — and to find it, in this film's bleak mirror, implicated in the same fantasy it depicts.

It remains one of the essential American films: a work that earns its discomfort, inhabits its darkness without flinching, and emerges with something true about the country that made it.

Review by Carrie Gorringe  ·  Published 1995  ·  Nitrate Online
